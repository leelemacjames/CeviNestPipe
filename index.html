A Thought Experiment

What Happens If You Fall Through the Earth?

A journey from a lift shaft to Newton's shell theorem, the harmonic oscillator, and the virial theorem — the hidden energy balance of every orbit in the universe.

Imagine you could drill a tunnel straight through the centre of the Earth. You step in. There's no air resistance, no friction. You fall. The question is: what happens next?

Think first

Before reading on, commit to an answer. Do you (a) accelerate all the way to the centre and crash into the far side, (b) reach the centre and stop, or (c) something else entirely?

The answer is (c). You oscillate. You fall through the centre, decelerate on the other side, come momentarily to rest at the surface on the far side, and then fall back. Back and forth, endlessly. The motion is simple harmonic — exactly the same as a mass on a spring.

This is a strange result. Gravity gets stronger as you approach a massive body, doesn't it? So why would you slow down before reaching the centre? The answer lies in one of Newton's most beautiful results.

The Key Insight

Newton's Shell Theorem

Newton proved two facts about spherical shells of uniform density:

Fact 1: A uniform spherical shell exerts zero gravitational force on any particle inside it. Every pull from one side is exactly cancelled by the pull from the other.

Fact 2: A uniform spherical shell attracts a particle outside it as if all the shell's mass were concentrated at its centre.

These two facts together have a remarkable consequence. When you are at radius $r$ inside the Earth (assuming uniform density $\rho$), all the mass in shells above you — the entire spherical shell from $r$ to $R_{\oplus}$ — exerts no net force on you. Only the mass below you matters, and by Fact 2, it acts as if concentrated at the centre.

Newton's Shell Theorem

Blue: the mass below you, which does pull you toward the centre. Grey: the shells above you, which exert zero net force. As you move inward, the active mass shrinks as $r^3$.

The Calculation

How Density Becomes Force

The mass enclosed within radius $r$ is the volume of a sphere times the density:

Enclosed mass
$$M(r) = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho$$

The gravitational force on a particle of mass $m$ at this radius, by Fact 2, is:

$$F = -\frac{GM(r)\,m}{r^2} = -\frac{G \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho \cdot m}{r^2} = -\frac{4\pi G\rho}{3}\,m\,r$$

Look at what happened. The $r^3$ from the enclosed mass and the $r^2$ in the denominator of Newton's law of gravitation leave a single power of $r$. The force is proportional to displacement from the centre:

Hooke's law — from gravity!
$$F = -kr, \qquad k = \frac{4\pi G\rho\, m}{3}$$

This is exactly Hooke's law: $F = -kr$. The "spring constant" is set by the density of the Earth and the gravitational constant. Inside a uniform sphere, gravity behaves like a spring.

The shell theorem translates a uniform density into a linear restoring force. Gravity inside a sphere is a harmonic oscillator.

IB Physics SL/HL The period of this oscillation is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k} = 2\pi\sqrt{3/(4\pi G\rho)}$. For Earth's mean density ($\rho \approx 5500\;\text{kg/m}^3$), this gives $T \approx 84$ minutes — the same as a low-Earth orbit. That's not a coincidence.

From Falling to Orbiting

The Breathing Orbit

Now instead of dropping straight through, imagine throwing the particle sideways. It doesn't fall through the centre — it orbits. But the orbit isn't perfectly circular (unless you throw at exactly the right speed). A slightly eccentric orbit breathes: the distance oscillates between a closest approach and a farthest point.

We can track this breathing through the moment of inertia about the force centre:

$$I = mr^2$$

For a circular orbit, $I$ is constant. For an eccentric orbit, $I$ oscillates — and the frequency of that oscillation tells us something deep about the force law.

IB Physics HL Differentiate $I = mr^2$ twice:

$$\dot{I} = 2mr\dot{r}, \qquad \ddot{I} = 2m(\dot{r}^2 + r\ddot{r})$$

Now substitute the radial equation of motion. In polar coordinates, $m\ddot{r} = f(r) + L^2/(mr^3)$, where $L$ is the conserved angular momentum. Multiplying by $r$ and substituting:

The breathing equation
$$\ddot{I} = 4T - 2rV'(r)$$

where $T = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{r}^2 + \frac{L^2}{2mr^2}$ is the total kinetic energy. The two terms on the right compete: kinetic energy drives expansion (the orbit inflates), while the potential gradient drives contraction (gravity pulls it back). An eccentric orbit breathes because these two effects alternate in dominance.

Orbit Breathing: Moment of Inertia

Left: the orbit for potential $V \propto r^n$. Right: $I(\theta) - I_0$, showing the breathing. For $n = -1$ (gravity), $\beta = 1$: one breath per orbit, closed ellipse. For $n = 2$ (harmonic), $\beta = 2$: two breaths, centred ellipse.

The Virial Theorem (in one paragraph)

Energy Balance from Breathing

The breathing equation has a beautiful consequence. Over one complete orbit, $I$ returns to its starting value, so $\langle\ddot{I}\rangle = 0$. For a power-law potential $V \propto r^n$, the term $rV'(r) = nV(r)$, and the breathing equation gives:

The virial theorem
$$2\langle T \rangle = n\langle V \rangle$$

For gravity ($n = -1$): $\langle T \rangle = -\frac{1}{2}\langle V \rangle$, so bound orbits have negative total energy. For the harmonic oscillator ($n = 2$): $\langle T \rangle = \langle V \rangle$ — equipartition, just as for a mass on a spring. The virial theorem falls straight out of the breathing equation. We will use it below.

The Payoff

Galaxy Rotation Curves

Now we can put the shell theorem to work on a real astrophysical problem. Consider a star orbiting at radius $r$ inside a galaxy with some density profile $\rho(r)$. The shell theorem tells us only the mass inside the orbit matters:

$$M(r) = 4\pi\int_0^r \rho(r')\,r'^2\,dr'$$

For a circular orbit at radius $r$, the gravitational pull must provide the centripetal acceleration:

$$\frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{GM(r)}{r^2} \implies v(r) = \sqrt{\frac{GM(r)}{r}}$$

This is the rotation curve: the orbital velocity as a function of radius. Its shape depends entirely on how $M(r)$ grows — which depends on $\rho(r)$.

Three limiting cases

Point mass ($M$ = const for $r > 0$): $v \propto r^{-1/2}$ — the Keplerian falloff. This is what the solar system does.
Uniform density ($\rho$ = const): $M \propto r^3$, so $v \propto r$ — velocity increases linearly. This is the harmonic oscillator regime from Section 2.
Isothermal halo ($\rho \propto 1/r^2$): $M \propto r$, so $v$ = const — a flat rotation curve.

In real galaxies, most of the visible light (stars, gas) is concentrated near the centre. If this visible matter were all there is, we would expect the rotation curve to rise in the inner region (where the star is inside an extended mass distribution) and then fall off as $1/\sqrt{r}$ in the outer region (where almost all the mass is inside the orbit). This is the Keplerian prediction.

What is actually observed is dramatically different. Rotation curves stay flat — $v \approx$ const — out to the largest radii that can be measured. This is the most striking evidence that either there is a large amount of unseen mass (dark matter) extending far beyond the visible disc, or the law of gravity itself is modified at these scales.

Galaxy Rotation Curves: Shell Theorem vs Observation

The solid curve shows $v(r) = \sqrt{GM(r)/r}$ computed from the shell theorem for the chosen density profile. The dashed line shows the observed flat rotation curve. The gap between them is the "missing mass problem."

IB Physics HL The shell theorem calculation above assumed spherical symmetry. Real galaxy discs are flattened, so the full calculation is more complex, but the essential physics is the same: $v(r)$ depends on $M(r)$, and $M(r)$ depends on $\rho(r)$. The flatness of the observed rotation curve implies $M(r) \propto r$ at large radii, which requires $\rho \propto 1/r^2$ — a density profile that extends far beyond the visible disc.

A-Level Further The virial theorem connects here too. For a flat rotation curve ($v$ = const), the potential is logarithmic: $V(r) \propto \ln r$. This doesn't fit the power-law virial theorem $2\langle T\rangle = n\langle V\rangle$ because $\ln r$ is not a power law. The logarithmic potential sits at the boundary of the virial classification — another hint that flat rotation curves represent a qualitative transition in gravitational dynamics.

The Thread

Let's trace the thread from beginning to end:

The shell theorem says that inside a uniform sphere, the gravitational force is proportional to distance: $F \propto r$. This is Hooke's law — a harmonic oscillator with $V \propto r^2$. So falling through the Earth produces simple harmonic motion.

For an orbit (sideways motion added), the same force law determines how the orbit breathes. The frequency parameter $\beta = \sqrt{n+2}$ (where $n$ is the potential exponent) tells us how many times the orbit breathes per revolution. For gravity ($n = -1$), $\beta = 1$: one breath, closed orbit. For the harmonic oscillator ($n = 2$), $\beta = 2$: two breaths, also closed.

The virial theorem falls out of the breathing equation by time-averaging: $2\langle T \rangle = n\langle V \rangle$. And the rotation curve of a galaxy is the shell theorem applied at every radius: $v(r) = \sqrt{GM(r)/r}$, with $M(r)$ determined by the density profile. The discrepancy between the predicted Keplerian decline and the observed flat curve is the most direct evidence for dark matter — or for the failure of Newtonian gravity at galactic scales.

Four results — the shell theorem, harmonic oscillation, the virial theorem, and galaxy rotation curves — are all manifestations of the same underlying mathematics: how the enclosed mass grows with radius, and what that implies for the shape of the effective potential.

The elevator through the Earth, the breathing orbit, and the galaxy rotation curve are not three separate problems. They are three faces of the same geometric fact — Newton's shell theorem.
