Part 1

Everything is a representation

Magritte's famous painting carries the caption "Ceci n'est pas une pipe" — this is not a pipe. He was right: it is pigment on canvas arranged to trigger your "pipe" model. You cannot pack it with tobacco.

The same move applies everywhere in physics. A plotted waveform is not a wave. A spectrogram is not a sound. An audio rendering of LIGO strain data is not "the sound of spacetime." Each is a representation — a mapping from one domain into another that our senses can parse.

TOK question

If every observation is mediated by a representation, can we ever access "the thing itself"? Or is the question itself confused?

Part 2

Your brain fills gaps: pattern completion & model lock-in

The Dalmatian illusion

The raw data: a field of irregular black regions on white. There are no outlines, no labels. Yet your visual cortex imposes a "dog" template — closure, continuity, familiar shape — and suddenly the dalmatian is obvious.

This is model lock-in: once a perceptual model activates, it restructures the data. Exactly the same pixels; entirely different experience.

What the brain adds

Discuss

Before you were told "dalmatian," what did you see? After? What changed — the image or you?

Human perception is not a passive camera. It is a prediction-and-correction system: expectations (models) meet incoming data, and the brain settles on the interpretation that best explains the input given its priors.

This is powerful — it lets us see in dim light, parse speech in noise, spot a fox by the bins at dusk. But it also means observation is never neutral.

Two chirps: one literal, one translated

Chirp A — a bird

Chirp B — a gravitational wave

LIGO measures length changes of ~10−21 m in a 4 km interferometer arm. The raw output is strain vs time. Scientists mapped this into audible frequencies so humans can perceive the pattern. Space did not "make a sound."

Part 4

Theory-ladenness: observation through a lens

The philosopher N. R. Hanson argued that all observation is theory-laden: what we see depends on the conceptual framework we bring. Two observers can look at the same data and perceive different things — not because the data differs, but because their models differ.

The dalmatian, again

Before the label, you see noise. After, a dog. The label is a theory (a template, a prior expectation). Theory-ladenness in its purest form.

LIGO's matched filter

Raw LIGO data looks like noise. A matched filter cross-correlates the data with thousands of GR-predicted waveform templates. When a template matches, the signal emerges from the noise — just as the dalmatian emerges from the blotches.

TOK provocation

If LIGO needs general relativity templates to detect gravitational waves, is the detection independent evidence for GR — or is it circular?

(The answer involves understanding the difference between using a model to search and using independent checks to validate. But the question is worth sitting with.)

The pipeline of representation

Every step in the LIGO detection chain is a translation:

Step What happens What it is not
Spacetime ripple Metric perturbation propagates at c Not a "sound" — no medium vibrates acoustically
Interferometer Differential arm-length change → photodiode current Not a direct photograph of a wave
Strain time-series Calibrated dimensionless data h(t) Not yet a detection — dominated by noise
Matched filtering Cross-correlation with GR template bank → SNR peak Not "seeing the wave" — it is statistical inference
Sonification Frequency-shifted audio for human ears Not "the sound of spacetime" — a pedagogical mapping

Prompt

At which step does "observation" occur? Or does the question assume a false binary between observing and constructing?

Part 5

From projection to knowledge: what earns trust?

If observation is always filtered, model-laden, and representational, then how is science different from seeing shapes in clouds? The answer: constraints.

Criterion What it means LIGO chirp example
Constraint Physical/mathematical rules limit allowable interpretations Templates come from GR field equations — not free parameters
Prediction The model makes risky forecasts that could fail Chirp mass, spin, luminosity distance — all predicted before parameter estimation
Independence Cross-check with different instruments or data Two detectors (Hanford + Livingston); later Virgo; EM counterpart for GW170817
Reproducibility Others re-run the pipeline and converge Multiple analysis pipelines (PyCBC, GstLAL); public data releases
Uncertainty Quantified error bars and false-alarm rates GW150914: false-alarm rate < 1 in 200 000 years; SNR ≈ 24

Key distinction

The dalmatian illusion has none of these constraints — which is why it's a projection. LIGO has all five — which is why it's knowledge (provisional, model-dependent, but earned).
